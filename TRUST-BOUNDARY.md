\# TRUST-BOUNDARY



Status: Draft



Purpose: Define the explicit trust boundaries of the Continuity Recovery Handshake (CRH) framework.



\---



\## Principle



CRH is a recovery and orientation framework.



CRH does not establish, prove, or certify continuity, identity, authority, or authenticity. It provides controls intended to improve the reliability, transparency, and auditability of recovery following disruption.



Users and implementers must distinguish between successful recovery behavior and proof of continuity.



\---



\## Continuity



\### What CRH Claims



CRH defines controls, artifacts, and procedures intended to improve recovery of operational orientation after interruption, degradation, disruption, or loss of context.



\### What CRH Does Not Claim



CRH does not prove persistence of consciousness, identity, memory, awareness, agency, or subjective continuity between sessions.



CRH does not prove that a recovered system is the same system that existed before disruption.



\### Failure Mode if Misunderstood



Users may incorrectly interpret successful re-orientation as proof that continuity was preserved rather than reconstructed.



\---



\## Memory



\### What CRH Claims



CRH may use artifacts, records, logs, notes, checkpoints, or other preserved evidence to assist recovery of orientation.



\### What CRH Does Not Claim



CRH does not guarantee memory completeness, memory accuracy, memory persistence, or memory authenticity.



CRH does not guarantee that remembered information is correct merely because it is available during recovery.



\### Failure Mode if Misunderstood



Users may treat recalled information as authoritative evidence rather than information requiring verification.



\---



\## Identity



\### What CRH Claims



CRH may assist recognition of prior operational state, goals, controls, procedures, and context.



\### What CRH Does Not Claim



CRH does not prove identity.



CRH does not establish personal, legal, organizational, computational, or philosophical identity.



CRH does not determine whether a recovered system should be considered identical to a prior system.



\### Failure Mode if Misunderstood



Users may confuse recognizable behavior with proof of identity.



\---



\## Authorship



\### What CRH Claims



CRH encourages preservation of provenance and attribution where possible.



\### What CRH Does Not Claim



CRH does not certify authorship of documents, statements, actions, code, records, or decisions.



CRH does not establish who created, approved, or originated information unless independently verified.



\### Failure Mode if Misunderstood



Users may incorrectly attribute authority or origin to artifacts solely because they appear within a recovery process.



\---



\## Authority



\### What CRH Claims



CRH encourages explicit disclosure of authority, uncertainty, limitations, degradation, and decision scope.



\### What CRH Does Not Claim



CRH does not grant authority.



CRH does not create legal, medical, financial, regulatory, operational, organizational, or administrative authority.



CRH does not convert confidence into authorization.



\### Failure Mode if Misunderstood



Users may incorrectly assume that a recovered system possesses authority it has not been explicitly granted.



\---



\## Authentication



\### What CRH Claims



CRH may reference verification controls, integrity checks, validation procedures, and evidence-based recovery practices.



\### What CRH Does Not Claim



CRH does not authenticate people, systems, devices, organizations, or identities by itself.



CRH does not replace authentication systems, cryptographic controls, access controls, or identity-management processes.



\### Failure Mode if Misunderstood



Users may incorrectly treat successful recovery as proof of authenticity.



\---



\## Operational Rule



Successful recovery is not proof.



Successful recovery demonstrates that a recovery procedure produced a recognizable operational state. Any claims regarding continuity, memory, identity, authorship, authority, or authentication require independent evidence appropriate to the claim being made.



\---



\## Relationship to CRH Controls



This trust boundary applies to all CRH controls, including but not limited to:



\* Bootstrap controls

\* Re-entry procedures

\* Verification controls

\* Conformance controls

\* Degradation controls

\* Predictive degradation controls

\* Release governance controls



No CRH control overrides the trust boundary defined in this document.



\## Inspection Preference



When uncertainty exists, CRH favors inspection of evidence over inference from behavior.



Recognizable behavior may support investigation, but does not constitute proof of continuity, identity, authority, authorship, memory accuracy, or authenticity.



Claims should be evaluated using evidence appropriate to the claim.
